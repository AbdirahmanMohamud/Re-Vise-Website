4.1 Charge and Current

  1. I = ΔQ / Δt where I is current, Q is charge and t is time.
  2. Current: The rate of flow of charge. It can be defined with the following equation: I = ΔQ / Δt
  3. Where I is current measured in Amperes, Q is charge measured in Coulombs, and t is time measured in seconds.
  4. Current is the movement of electrons in metals and movement of ions in electrolytes.
  5. Metals:
  6. A metal is a structure of metal ions held together by a sea of delocalised electrons.
  7. The delocalised electrons are able to move throughout the metal lattice.
  8. The electric current in a metal is due to the movement of electrons.
  9. Electrolyte:
  10. In an electrolyte, electrons are unable to move freely.
  11. Instead, the current is due to the movement of ions.
  12. Current is measured in Amperes
  13. Ampere: One ampere is defined as the flow of one coulomb per second.
  14. Current is measured using an Ammeter that must be placed in series. Theoretically an ammeter should have negligible resistance. Negligible resistance means that p.d. across the ammeter is minimal/reduced so that it does not affect the readings.
  15. Conventional current flows from the positive terminal to the negative terminal. Whereas Electrons actually flow from the negative terminal to the positive terminal.
  16. Electric Charge: The total amount of current that is supplied over a certain period of time. It can be defined with the following equation: Q = I x t
  17. Charge is measured in Coulombs
  18. Coulomb: One coulomb (C) is defined as the amount of charge that passes in 1 second when the current is 1 ampere.
  19. Charge derived into its base units is A x s = As
  20. Elementary Charge: The elementary charge, e, is the electric charge carried by a single electron. It is 1.60 x 10-19 C
  21. The net charge on a particle or an object is quantised and a multiple of e
  22. An electron has charge −e and a proton a charge +e.
  23. To calculate the number of electrons transferred
  24. Charge (C) / elementary charge (1.6 x 10-19 C ) or simply Q / e.
  25. Kirchhoff’s First Law:
  26. The sum of the currents entering a point / junction is equal to the sum of the currents leaving (the same point)
  27. ∑ Iin = ∑ Iout
  28. This is a consequence of the conservation of charge, as charge cannot be created or destroyed/ used or lost.
  29. Mean Drift Velocity of electrons:
  • The displacement travelled by electrons per unit time along the length of the wire.
  • Overtime, the free electrons drift towards the positive end of the supply along the length of the wire.
  • The free electrons also make collisions with metal ions and this gives them a random velocity.
  1. I = nAve
  2. Where ‘I’ is current measured in Amperes.
  3. n is the number density of charge carriers per unit volume measured in m-3
  4. A is the cross-sectional area measured in m2
  5. v is the mean drift velocity of charge carriers measured in ms-1
  6. e is the elementary charge of one electron measured in Coulombs (1.6 x 10-19)
  7. v ∝ I, Double the current, double the mean drift velocity.
  8. v ∝ 1/A, Double the cross-sectional area, half the mean drift velocity.
  9. v ∝ 1/n, Double the number density, half the mean drift velocity.
  10. If the current and number density are constant (and e is always constant), then the mean drift velocity ∝ 1/A , therefore the smaller the cross-sectional area of wire, the greater the mean drift velocity and vice versa.
  11. For a cylindrical wire, if the radius halves, the cross sectional area will decrease by a factor of 4, so the mean drift velocity must increase by a factor of 4.
  12. Material Categories:
  13. Conductors such as copper have a very high number density, semiconductors are lower, and insulators are very low.
  14. Since v ∝ 1/n, conductors will have very low mean drift velocities and insulators will have very high mean drift velocities.
  15. This is because the current is the same, so the electron mean drift velocity will increase in order to pass the same charge in the same time.

  17. Threshold Voltage: The minimum forward voltage value across the terminals of a diode at which the diode will start to conduct current. Most diodes require a threshold voltage of about 0.6V

  19. I-V characteristics of a Diode/LED.
  20. A diode/LED only allows current to flow in one direction.
  21. When connected in the forward bias direction, they give a low resistance.

  2. The sum of the currents entering a point / junction is equal to the sum of the currents leaving (the same point)

4.4 Waves 1

  1. Progressive Waves: is a transfer of energy, as a result of oscillations of the source
  2. Progressive waves are produced by a series of vibrations or oscillations of electric and magnetic fields. Progressive waves transfer energy from one place to another without transferring any material. The transfer of energy is the same direction as the direction of the wave is travelling. Since waves carry energy away, the source of the wave loses energy.
  3. Longitudinal Waves: A wave where displacement/oscillation (of particles) is parallel to the direction of energy transfer.
  4. Example: Sound waves
  5. Longitudinal waves have areas of compression and rarefaction:
  6. A compression is a region in a longitudinal wave where the particles are closest together.
  7. A rarefaction is a region in a longitudinal wave where the particles are furthest apart.
  8. Transverse Waves: A wave where displacement/oscillation (of particles) is perpendicular to the direction of energy transfer.
  9. Examples: surface water waves, waves on a string, electromagnetic waves.
  10. Transverse waves can be represented on a graph in two different ways:
  11. On a displacement – distance graph
  12. This graph shows the displacement of many particles from different points, at an instant in time, from their equilibrium positions.
  13. On a displacement – time graph
  14. This graph shows the displacement of one particle from its equilibrium position, measured at the same position but at different times.
  15. Displacement, x: distance moved from the equilibrium position by a point/particle on a wave. Measured in metres (m).
  16. Amplitude, A: Maximum displacement from the equilibrium position (caused by wave motion). Measured in metres (m).
  17. Wavelength, λ: distance between (neighbouring) identical points/points with same phase (on the wave). Measured in metres (m).
  18. Period, T: The time taken for a whole cycle/oscillation to complete. Measured in seconds (s)
  19. Phase difference: How far through a cycle one point is compared to the other. The comparison is between two points on the same wave of the same frequency, or on the same wave. Measured in degrees or radians.
  20. Frequency, f: number of complete cycles passing a point per unit time/second. Measured in Hertz (Hz)
